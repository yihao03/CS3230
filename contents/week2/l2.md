## Deriving the tight bound

**Substitution:** Let $n = 2^k$, so $k = \log_2 n$, and define $S(k) = T(2^k)$.

The recurrence becomes:

$$S(k) = S(k-1) + 2^{\sqrt{k}}$$

**Unrolling:**

$$S(k) = S(0) + \sum_{i=1}^{k} 2^{\sqrt{i}}$$

So the key is evaluating $\sum_{i=1}^{k} 2^{\sqrt{i}}$.

### Estimating the sum

**Integral approximation:** Substitute $u = \sqrt{x}$, $dx = 2u\,du$:

$$\int_1^k 2^{\sqrt{x}}\,dx = 2\int_1^{\sqrt{k}} u \cdot 2^u\,du$$

Integration by parts gives:

$$\int u \cdot 2^u\,du = \frac{u \cdot 2^u}{\ln 2} - \frac{2^u}{(\ln 2)^2}$$

Evaluating at $\sqrt{k}$, the dominant term is $\frac{\sqrt{k} \cdot 2^{\sqrt{k}}}{\ln 2}$, so the integral is $\Theta(\sqrt{k} \cdot 2^{\sqrt{k}})$.

**Why the last term alone isn't enough:** The ratio of consecutive terms $2^{\sqrt{i}}/2^{\sqrt{i-1}} = 2^{\sqrt{i}-\sqrt{i-1}} \approx 2^{1/(2\sqrt{i})} \to 1$, so the terms don't decay geometrically â€” many terms near $i = k$ contribute significantly. Specifically, for $i = k - j$:

$$2^{\sqrt{k-j}} \approx 2^{\sqrt{k}} \cdot 2^{-j/(2\sqrt{k})}$$

Summing the geometric-like tail gives a factor of $\Theta(\sqrt{k})$ multiplying the largest term.

### Result

Substituting back $k = \log_2 n$:

$$\boxed{T(n) = \Theta\!\left(\sqrt{\log n}\;\cdot\; 2^{\sqrt{\log n}}\right)}$$
